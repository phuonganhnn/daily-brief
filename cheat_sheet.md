# Koru Cheat Sheet

A working reference for a Vietnamese sell-side / principal-investing analyst.
This is meant to live next to your daily brief — the brief tells you *what to
think about*, this sheet tells you *what vocabulary to think in* and gives
you canned questions to start from when you're stuck.

Update it whenever you learn a new term or see a new mechanic in the wild.

---

## 1 · Frameworks (in your preference order)

### Second-order thinking
The reader's #1 muscle. Don't ask "what does this mean" — ask "what does
this *force* somebody else to do, and what does *that* make inevitable?"

**Template questions:**
- If A is now true, what does it force competitor B to do within 12 months?
- Who is the non-obvious loser here? (Hint: usually a supplier or distributor.)
- What does this make *cheap* that wasn't cheap yesterday?
- Whose cost of capital just went up? Whose just went down?
- What's the headline 18 months from now if this trend continues unchecked?

**Worked example.** Headline: "Vingroup announces VinFast US factory delay
to 2027." First-order: bad for VinFast. Second-order: (a) the Vietnamese
supplier ecosystem that tooled up for the original timeline now has 18 months
of idle capacity → distress → consolidation play; (b) the local debt holders
of those suppliers face mark-downs → which banks have the concentrated exposure?
(c) the labour pool VinFast was pulling in Hai Phong now stays available → who
opens a factory there next? That's where deals come from.

### Reverse engineering
Work backward from observed price/structure to implied assumptions.
Faster than building a model. Forces honest comparison.

**Template questions:**
- At this price, what peak revenue / EBITDA does the buyer need by year 5?
- What growth rate is implied if entry multiple = exit multiple?
- What synergy quantum is implicit? (Premium × target sales = synergy NPV
  the buyer expects to extract.)
- At what assumed WACC does this deal clear hurdle rate?
- What exit multiple is the sponsor underwriting? Compare to current public comps.

**Worked example.** Buyer pays 12x EBITDA for a VN F&B chain trading at
~7x public comp. The 5x premium = ~$50M on a $10M EBITDA target. Either
(a) buyer expects EBITDA to double in 3 years (50% CAGR) which is heroic,
(b) buyer expects to re-rate via cross-border acquisition at exit, or
(c) buyer is paying for a control premium that should never be modeled
into base case. Which is it? The press release usually tells you.

### Porter's Five Forces (use sparingly, only when sector dynamics matter)
Buyers · Suppliers · New entrants · Substitutes · Rivalry. Most useful as
a checklist when you're new to a sector — pick the one or two forces that
actually move the needle (most sectors only have 1-2 binding constraints,
not all 5).

### Cohort & unit economics
For consumer / subscription / repeat-purchase businesses. Always ask:
- What is the unit? (one customer? one outlet? one transaction? one truck-trip?)
- What does it cost to acquire one unit?
- What does the unit pay you over its lifetime?
- How does this month's cohort behave vs the cohort 12 months ago?
- Where does the cohort curve flatten — and is that flat part profitable
  or loss-making?

### Jobs-to-be-done
For positioning / M&A rationale stories. "What job is the customer hiring
this product to do?" Useful when a deal looks weird — usually the buyer
is buying a *capability* to serve a job they're already failing at.

## 2 · Deal mechanics vocabulary

The terms that show up in deal press releases and that you should be able
to define cold. Keep adding to this as you encounter new ones.

**Anti-embarrassment clause** — if the buyer flips the asset within X
months at a higher price, the seller gets a % of the upside. Common in
distressed sales where sellers fear they're leaving money on the table.

**Anti-dilution (full ratchet vs weighted average)** — if a future round
prices below the current round, earlier investors' conversion price
re-prices down. Full ratchet = all the way down to the new low (brutal
to founders). Weighted average = blend (the standard).

**Bolt-on** — a small acquisition added to a platform investment.
Usually justified by EBITDA arbitrage (small targets trade lower) and
synergy capture.

**Buy-and-build** — the macro version of bolt-on. Sponsor buys a
platform, then bolts on 5-15 smaller competitors over the hold period
to build scale and re-rate at exit. TPG, Carlyle, Warburg love this.

**Carry** — the % of fund profits the GP keeps above the hurdle. Standard
2/20 = 2% mgmt fee + 20% carry. Some growth funds run 1.5/15.

**Catch-up** — after the LP gets their hurdle return, the GP "catches up"
to their full carry % before the standard split kicks in. Usually 100%
catch-up to the GP, then 80/20 standard split.

**Completion accounts** — actual closing balance sheet is finalized
post-close, and price adjusts. The opposite of locked box.

**Drag-along** — majority shareholders can force minorities to sell on
the same terms. Critical for clean exits.

**Earn-out** — part of the price is contingent on the target hitting
post-close performance milestones (revenue, EBITDA, regulatory approval).
Bridges valuation gaps; common in healthcare and tech.

**EBITDA bridge** — the walk from reported EBITDA to "adjusted" EBITDA.
Sellers always want add-backs for "one-time" items (founder comp, legal,
non-recurring marketing). Buyers always push back. Where the bridge
lands is half the deal negotiation.

**Enterprise value** — equity value + net debt. The number you compare
to multiples. Always check what's in net debt — pension liabilities,
operating leases, customer prepayments can swing it 20%.

**Locked box** — closing balance sheet is set at signing, not closing.
Buyer takes the risk of any deterioration between signing and closing,
seller gets a guaranteed price. Increasingly the European/Asian default.

**MAC (material adverse change)** — clause that lets the buyer walk if
the target's business deteriorates significantly between signing and
closing. Famously hard to enforce — see LVMH/Tiffany 2020.

**Management roll-over** — the existing management team rolls some of
their proceeds back into equity in the new entity. Aligns incentives.
Standard in PE buy-outs.

**MBO / MBI** — management buy-out (existing team buys the business with
PE backing) vs management buy-in (external team comes in). MBOs preserve
operational continuity; MBIs are higher-risk, higher-return.

**Pre-money / Post-money** — pre = valuation before the new money goes in;
post = pre + new money. Always clarify which is being quoted, especially
in growth rounds.

**Ratchet** — sliding scale of who gets what equity at exit, depending
on performance vs targets. Often given to founders as upside if they
hit hurdles; sometimes to sponsors as protection if they don't.

**Recap (recapitalization)** — sponsor refinances the target's debt (or
takes a dividend) without selling. Way to crystallize returns mid-hold.
Aggressive recaps are how PE firms get accused of stripping companies.

**ROFR / ROFO** — right of first refusal / first offer. Existing shareholders
get to match (ROFR) or pre-empt (ROFO) any proposed sale. Slows down
auctions but protects strategic relationships.

**Strategic premium** — what a strategic buyer pays above what a financial
buyer would, justified by synergies. Usually 20-40% of standalone EV.
Disappears in cyclical downturns when corporates pull back.

**Tag-along** — minority shareholders can force themselves into a sale on
the same terms as the majority. The mirror of drag-along; protects minorities.

**VDR (virtual data room)** — where the seller dumps everything for the
buyer's due diligence. Quality of the VDR is usually correlated with
quality of the seller's prep — and inversely with how much the buyer
will trust management going forward.

**Vendor loan / vendor financing** — seller finances part of the price
themselves (subordinated note, typically 5-7 years, mid-single-digit
coupon). Bridges financing gaps in tight credit markets and signals
seller confidence in the business.

**Working capital peg** — agreed normalized working capital level at
close. If actual is below the peg at closing, price gets reduced;
if above, increased. The single most-litigated post-close item.

## 3 · The "real KPI" by sector

The metric the operator actually obsesses over — not what shows up in
the public deck.

### Consumer & Retail (FMCG, retail chains, F&B)
- **NRPO/W** — net revenue per outlet per week. The single number that
  decides if a chain is healthy.
- **GT vs MT split** — general trade (mom-and-pop) vs modern trade
  (supermarkets, convenience). VN is still ~70% GT, which is a moat
  for incumbents with feet-on-street distribution.
- **Trade spend %** — promo + slotting + listing fees as % of gross sales.
  In VN FMCG this can be 15-25%. Hidden in "rebates" line.
- **Like-for-like growth** — same-store sales vs prior period, stripping
  out new openings. The honest growth number.
- **Stock cover days** — for a distributor, the make-or-break number.

### Healthcare (pharma, biotech, hospitals, clinics)
- **Hospitals: bed occupancy %** (>75% = healthy), **ALOS** (average length
  of stay — shorter is better for margin), **case mix index** (proxy for
  acuity, drives revenue per bed), **payor mix** (% private vs social
  insurance — private = better cash flow).
- **Pharma: prescription decile** (top 10% of doctors generate 50%+ of
  scrips — sales force ROI = how well you cover them), **gross-to-net**
  (list price minus rebates and channel fees — the real revenue).
- **Clinics chains: same-store visit growth + revenue per visit + doctor
  retention rate**. Doctor turnover above 15% kills the model.
- **Biotech: cash runway in months + nearest readout date**. Everything
  else is noise until the readout.

### B2B manufacturing & industrials
- **Capacity utilization %** (>80% = healthy, >90% = at the wall)
- **OEE (overall equipment effectiveness)** = availability × performance × quality
- **Tonnes per shift / units per shift** — the operator's daily number
- **Customer concentration** — top 5 customers as % of revenue. Above 40%
  is a red flag for any acquirer.

### Hard assets (infra, energy, hospitality real estate, logistics RE)
- **DSCR (debt service coverage ratio)** — for any leveraged asset, this
  is the covenant. Below 1.2x = trouble.
- **Capacity factor / load factor** — for power assets, the % of nameplate
  actually delivered.
- **Cap rate** — for real estate, NOI / EV. The honest yield.
- **WALT (weighted average lease term)** — for commercial RE, the
  contracted income runway.

### Financial services (banks, insurance, fintech, NBFC)
- **NIM (net interest margin)** — banks; the spread on their assets.
- **CIR (cost-to-income ratio)** — banks; under 45% = efficient.
- **NPL %** — banks; under 2% = healthy in VN context.
- **CASA ratio** — current and savings account % of deposits. High
  CASA = cheap funding = better NIM.
- **Combined ratio** — insurers; under 100% = underwriting profit.
- **CAC payback months** — fintech / NBFC; how long until the customer
  has paid back the cost to acquire them. Under 12 months = world class.

### Supply chain & logistics (3PL, cold chain, ports, last mile)
- **Truck/asset utilization %** — empty miles kill the unit economics.
  Above 75% loaded = healthy.
- **Dwell time** — at ports/warehouses; minutes a truck waits. Bigger
  drag on margin than fuel.
- **Claim ratio** — % of shipments with damage/loss claims. Above 1%
  for cold chain = process broken.
- **Cost per stop / cost per delivery** — for last mile, the only number.
- **Density** — number of stops per route per km. The whole game.

### Vietnam-specific
- **CapEx-heavy commodity / paper / cement / aggregates: $/tonne delivered radius.**
  Logistics cost erodes margin past ~150km; you sell where you produce.
  This is your favourite example for a reason — it generalizes to
  anything heavy-and-cheap.

## 4 · Quick valuation reference (typical Asia ranges)

| Sector | EV/Revenue | EV/EBITDA | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| FMCG (branded) | 2-4x | 12-18x | Premiums for distribution moats |
| Modern retail | 0.7-1.5x | 8-14x | Discounts for capex intensity |
| Hospitals | 2-4x | 10-16x | Premium for chain scalability |
| Generic pharma | 1.5-3x | 8-12x | Discount for pricing pressure |
| Specialty pharma / biotech | 4-8x | 15-25x | If profitable |
| B2B manufacturing | 0.8-1.5x | 6-10x | Sensitive to customer concentration |
| Cold-chain logistics | 1.5-2.5x | 9-13x | Premium for asset-light models |
| Banks | n/a | n/a | Use P/B (1.0-2.0x) and P/E (8-12x) |
| Fintech / payments | 3-8x | 15-30x | If proven unit economics |
| Renewable IPP | n/a | 8-12x | Or DCF — IRR-driven |
| Commercial RE | n/a | n/a | Cap rate 6-9% in VN |

These are *gut check* ranges, not gospel. Always anchor to the most recent
real comp transaction in the same country.

## 5 · Canned starting questions when you're stuck

When you stare at a deal news story and don't know how to think about it,
pick one of these to start:

**For an M&A announcement:**
1. Who's the seller, and why are they selling now?
2. What multiple is implied — and how does that compare to the most recent
   public comp transaction in this sector and country?
3. What's in the EBITDA bridge that we can't see from the press release?
4. If this deal closes at this price, what's the implied 5-year exit IRR
   the buyer is underwriting? Is that realistic given their fund hurdle?
5. Who are the *non-obvious* losers from this deal?

**For a fundraising round:**
1. Pre-money or post-money quoted?
2. What's the round dilution? (New money / post = ownership the new
   investors got.)
3. At this implied valuation, what revenue does the company need in 24
   months to justify a flat next round?
4. Is the lead investor in their normal stage and check size, or stretching?
   (Stretching = signal of conviction OR signal of FOMO.)

**For a sector / business news story:**
1. What does this make true for the *unit*? (per customer / per outlet
   / per truck-trip)
2. Whose moat just got wider? Whose just got narrower?
3. What does the market look like 24 months from now if this trend keeps
   compounding at the rate the article implies?
4. Who do I know running a company in this space — would they care about
   this?

**For a regulatory / macro story:**
1. Who is the *intended* beneficiary of this rule?
2. Who is the *actual* beneficiary?
3. What capital flow does this redirect, and on what timeline?
4. Which listed VN names are most exposed?

## 6 · Resources to build commercial sense over time

**Books worth reading slowly:**
- *The Outsiders* (William Thorndike) — capital allocation patterns
- *Pitch the Perfect Investment* (Sonkin & Johnson) — how to actually
  build a thesis end-to-end
- *Investment Banking* (Rosenbaum & Pearl) — the mechanics bible
- *Capital Returns* (Edward Chancellor, ed.) — why capital cycles drive
  more deal outcomes than management does

**Podcasts:**
- *Acquired* — long deep dives on famous deals; great for reverse-engineering
  rationale years after the fact
- *In the Money* (Stacy-Marie Ishmael) — punchy markets/macro

**Substacks:**
- *Net Interest* (Marc Rubinstein) — financial services with sharp framing
- *Vietnam Weekly* (Michael Tatarski) — VN political/economic context
- *Doomberg* — energy & commodities, contrarian
- *Drew Dickson / Albert Bridge Capital* — value investing with discipline

**People to coffee:**
- Any associate at Quadria, Mekong, VinaCapital, Dragon Capital
- Corporate finance partners at BDA, Lazard SEA, Cantor Vietnam
- ASEAN-focused MDs at any of the bulge brackets — they love teaching
  smart analysts
